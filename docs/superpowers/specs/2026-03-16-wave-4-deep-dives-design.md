# Wave 4 Deep Dives — Content Expansion Design

## Overview

12 new Deep Dive articles for cnjianghu, expanding beyond the 30-article Launch Slate. Every article draws from a **previously untouched dossier**, opening 12 new content territories across all 6 pillars.

**Core principle:** Each article is a **story, phenomenon, or contradiction** — never a concept definition. Deep Dives are not long glossary entries. The test: can you picture a specific person, place, or moment?

## Writing Structure

All Deep Dives follow the established core structure: **Hook → Misconception/Reality → Significance → Reframe**

Within this structure, the editorial approach emerges naturally from each topic's content. The project's 30 published articles demonstrate at least 6 distinct narrative strategies:

- **Scene-opening** — a vivid moment pulls the reader in, then unpacks deeper mechanics (e.g., hot water, breakfast, public leisure)
- **Misconception correction** — what foreigners assume vs. what's actually happening (e.g., guochao, livestream rooms)
- **Character-driven** — follow a specific person through an experience (e.g., Mr. Li's favor ledger, the host's smile)
- **Translation/language gap** — a word's untranslatability exposes cultural distance (e.g., "What Involution Loses in Translation")
- **Data/contrast hook** — a surprising number or juxtaposition drives curiosity (e.g., 100 million dancers, blind boxes)
- **Timeline/evolution** — historical thread leads to contemporary meaning (e.g., hanfu across 3000 years, myth revival)

Each article below notes a **suggested narrative strategy**, but this is guidance — the final approach should follow the content's natural shape during writing.

## The 12 Articles

### Taste & Life

#### 1. Sweet or Savory? In China, This Question Starts Wars

- **Dossier:** Regional Taste Systems (untouched)
- **Pillar:** taste-life
- **Slug:** `sweet-or-savory-china-food-wars`
- **Narrative strategy:** Scene-opening + data/contrast — open with a specific online flame war (e.g., a Weibo zongzi poll that got 300 million votes), then unpack why food arguments are really identity arguments.
- **Story:** China's great food arguments — sweet vs savory zongzi, who invented hotpot (Sichuan or Chongqing), where does good rice end. These aren't food debates. They're identity wars where region, migration memory, and belonging collide on the internet every holiday season. Online flame wars, family dinner standoffs, migrant nostalgia for "their" taste in a new city.
- **Why it works:** Every foreigner who's eaten Chinese food has heard "Chinese food isn't one cuisine." This article shows WHY that matters — through the human stories of people defending their hometown's taste.

#### 2. Two Billion Dumplings in Forty-Eight Hours

- **Dossier:** Festival Foods (untouched)
- **Pillar:** taste-life
- **Slug:** `two-billion-dumplings-in-48-hours`
- **Narrative strategy:** Data/contrast + character-driven — the sheer number (billions of dumplings, 48 hours, 300 million families) is the hook, then zoom into what actually happens inside one kitchen when three generations fold together.
- **Story:** Every Lunar New Year, hundreds of millions of families converge on the same activity within the same 48-hour window. The kitchen becomes a negotiation space — who leads the recipe, who films for Douyin, who folds wrong. Not what festival food IS, but what happens when a nation prepares food simultaneously and why the kitchen is where family hierarchy, generational gaps, and belonging get renegotiated.
- **Why it works:** The scale is staggering and immediately graspable. The article moves from macro (national logistics) to micro (one kitchen, one family) without sentimentality.

### Digital China

- **Dossier:** Bullet Comments and Collective Spectatorship (untouched)
- **Pillar:** digital-china
- **Slug:** `words-floating-across-screen-not-subtitles`
- **Narrative strategy:** Misconception correction — foreigners see danmu as visual noise or broken subtitles. The article reveals it as a collective viewing ritual that turns solitary screen time into shared experience.

#### 4. The Night Xiaohongshu Became American

- **Dossier:** Cross-Border App Encounters (untouched)
- **Pillar:** digital-china
- **Slug:** `the-night-xiaohongshu-became-american`
- **Narrative strategy:** Timeline/evolution — chronological account of a specific event (January 2025), with cultural analysis woven through the timeline.

### Living China

#### 5. ¥25K a Month in Shanghai, and She Still Left

- **Dossier:** Tier-System Lives (untouched)
- **Pillar:** living-china
- **Slug:** `25k-a-month-in-shanghai-and-she-still-left`
- **Narrative strategy:** Character-driven — follow one composite person's decision arc (why she left, what changed, what she lost), then zoom out to the broader reverse-migration pattern.
- **Story:** Reverse migration — young professionals leaving tier-1 cities for tier-3/4 — isn't failure. It's recalibration. What changes when you trade a two-hour commute for a ten-minute walk? What do you gain when you stop competing for the one version of "a good life" everyone agreed on? And what do you lose that you didn't expect?
- **Why it works:** The tier system is invisible to foreigners but organizes Chinese life profoundly. This story enters through one person's decision, not a sociological explanation.

#### 6. Graduate by 22, Marry by 28, Don't Ask Why

- **Dossier:** Youth Culture and Life Scripts (untouched)
- **Pillar:** living-china
- **Slug:** `graduate-by-22-marry-by-28`
- **Narrative strategy:** Data/contrast hook — open with the milestone list itself (the "spreadsheet"), then show real people navigating, bending, or breaking it.
- **Story:** The Chinese life script isn't written down, but everyone knows the milestones — and every family dinner, every Spring Festival reunion, every casual question from an aunt reminds you. What happens when a generation raised on these milestones starts to edit the spreadsheet instead of following it? The tension between filial expectation and personal desire, played out in career choices, housing decisions, and group chat silences.
- **Why it works:** Universally relatable (pressure to follow a script) but culturally specific (the Chinese version has its own milestones, pressures, and escape valves).
- **Boundary note:** Existing article "The Invisible Curriculum of a Chinese Wedding" covers marriage economics, xiangqin pipeline, and bride price. This article focuses on the FULL life script — gaokao, graduate school, first job, home purchase timing — with marriage as ONE milestone in the spreadsheet, not the main thread. Avoid marriage economics territory.

### Aesthetic China

#### 7. Why a Chinese Garden Looks Different Every Ten Steps

- **Dossier:** Architecture and Spatial Philosophy (untouched)
- **Pillar:** aesthetic-china
- **Slug:** `why-chinese-garden-looks-different-every-ten-steps`
- **Narrative strategy:** Data/contrast — the observable fact (the same garden looks completely different every ten steps) is the hook. Then unpack the design principles: framing, concealment, sequential revelation. Connect to how this spatial logic persists in contemporary Chinese design.
- **Story:** A Chinese garden can't be captured in one photograph. It doesn't exist from one angle. Doors frame views that change with each step, corridors withhold then reveal, windows become paintings. This design philosophy — space as choreography, not vista — quietly shapes how China builds everything from classical temples to contemporary shopping malls. Walk through one, and you understand a different relationship between body and space.
- **Why it works:** Any tourist who's visited a Chinese garden has felt this but couldn't articulate it. The article makes the invisible design logic visible.

#### 8. Why White Walls Make Some Chinese Grandmothers Nervous

- **Dossier:** Chinese Color Systems (untouched)
- **Pillar:** aesthetic-china
- **Slug:** `why-white-walls-make-grandmothers-nervous`
- **Narrative strategy:** Misconception correction + data/contrast — foreigners see red everywhere and think "lucky color." The reality is a complete color grammar where white, yellow, green, and gold each carry social weight that shifts by context. Open with a concrete collision (e.g., a foreigner wrapping a gift in white paper, or Muji's all-white store in a culture where white = mourning).
- **Story:** White = mourning in traditional Chinese color code. But white also = Apple stores, modern cafes, Muji aesthetics. Chinese people navigate both meanings simultaneously — the same color reads differently depending on context, generation, and setting. Red isn't just "lucky" — it operates differently at a wedding vs. a funeral vs. a brand logo. Yellow carries imperial weight. Green can signal infidelity. The Chinese color system is a complete social grammar that foreigners unknowingly violate every time they wrap a gift or choose an outfit.
- **Why it works:** Color is something every foreigner encounters in China — red everywhere at New Year, gold on temples, the missing white at weddings. The gap between "I see these colors" and "I understand what they mean" is exactly where this article lives.
- **Adjacency note:** No overlap with existing content. "The Box That Built a World" (Pop Mart/blind boxes) covers a different aesthetic-china territory entirely.

### Mind China

#### 9. My Doctor Said I'm Damp

- **Dossier:** TCM as a Way of Seeing (untouched)
- **Pillar:** mind-china
- **Slug:** `my-doctor-said-im-damp`
- **Narrative strategy:** Character-driven + misconception correction — follow one person's TCM clinic visit as the entry point, then reveal how two medical worldviews coexist inside the same person without contradiction.
- **Story:** For most Chinese people, TCM isn't a medical choice. It's a worldview operating system. A 25-year-old tech worker walks into a TCM clinic, gets told she's "damp" (湿气重), and isn't surprised — she already suspected it. TCM language (上火, 湿气, 寒凉) shapes how people talk about their bodies, choose foods, and give advice, even when they also trust hospitals for serious illness. Two systems coexist inside one person without contradiction.
- **Why it works:** "Drink hot water" is already the site's most-shared concept. This article goes deeper — into why the body logic behind it feels completely normal to a generation raised on smartphones.
- **Boundary note:** Existing hot-water article covers the history and mechanics of body-temperature logic (shanghuo, warming/cooling foods). This article covers how TCM as a *worldview operating system* coexists with modern medicine in the same person — the dual-system phenomenon, not the temperature theory.

#### 10. Why There Is No Fourth Floor in This Building

- **Dossier:** Fate, Luck, and Timing (untouched)
- **Pillar:** mind-china
- **Slug:** `why-no-fourth-floor-in-this-building`
- **Narrative strategy:** Scene-opening + data/contrast — open with the observable detail (elevator buttons: 1, 2, 3, 5...), then expand into the entire auspicious number/timing system through specific human stories.
- **Story:** Buildings skip the 4th floor. Weddings cluster on 8/8. A business owner won't sign a contract on certain days. Phone numbers with repeated 8s sell for thousands. Auspicious timing in China isn't superstition — it's a shared social grammar that structures time, space, and decision-making. The real-world consequences: developers renumbering floors, couples rescheduling weddings around date availability, phone number auctions reaching six figures.
- **Why it works:** Every foreigner who's visited China has noticed the missing 4th floor. This article starts with that observable detail and opens into an entire world of timing logic.

### Future China

#### 11. Why 50 Million People Watch Chocolate Being Made

- **Dossier:** Manufacturing as Culture (untouched)
- **Pillar:** future-china
- **Slug:** `why-50-million-watch-chocolate-being-made`
- **Narrative strategy:** Data/contrast hook — the number itself is the hook (50 million viewers for a factory video?!), then explore the cultural pride, ASMR pleasure, and production aesthetics behind the phenomenon.
- **Story:** Factory tour videos are among the most-watched content on Chinese Douyin. A chocolate assembly line, a ceramics workshop, an EV body panel being stamped — viewed not as industry reporting, but as spectacle and satisfaction. The comment sections reveal a mix of pride ("this is what we can do"), ASMR pleasure, and genuine curiosity about how things work. What does it mean when a nation finds beauty in production itself?
- **Why it works:** Factory video culture is a uniquely Chinese internet phenomenon. Foreigners find it puzzling — the article explains the cultural confidence and aesthetic pleasure behind it.

#### 12. Your Face Opens the Front Door. Nobody Thinks That's Strange.

- **Dossier:** Smart City Habits (untouched)
- **Pillar:** future-china
- **Slug:** `your-face-opens-the-front-door`
- **Narrative strategy:** Character-driven + misconception correction — follow one resident through a full day's digital checkpoints (face scan → QR gym → subway code → delivery scan), then explore the gap between the Western surveillance framing and the lived experience of convenience.
- **Story:** Face scan to enter your apartment compound. QR code to check into the gym. Health code for the subway. In Chinese cities, daily movement leaves a digital trail so continuous that "privacy" as a Western framework barely maps onto the lived experience. But ask residents and most describe it in one word: convenient. The article follows a day in a typical resident's life, tracking every digital checkpoint, and explores the gap between how outsiders imagine it and how insiders experience it.
- **Why it works:** Smart city discourse in English is dominated by surveillance framing. This article offers the resident's-eye-view — neither naive nor dystopian.
- **Sourcing note:** Primary evidence likely T3-T4 (Sixth Tone features, Douyin resident commentary, academic ethnographies). Ground with T1 sources: Pew Research Center surveys on Chinese attitudes toward surveillance technology, academic studies on smart city adoption. This is the highest-difficulty sourcing article in the batch.
