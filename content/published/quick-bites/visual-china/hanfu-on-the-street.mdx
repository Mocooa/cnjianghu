The scene: A shopping district in Chengdu, Xi'an, or Hangzhou on a weekend afternoon.

**What you see:** A young woman walks past a milk tea shop wearing a flowing Ming dynasty-style dress with wide sleeves and an embroidered collar. Her friend wears a Tang dynasty-inspired top with a modern skirt. Neither is heading to a costume party or a photo studio. They are going shopping. Nobody around them looks surprised.

**What is happening:** 汉服 (hànfú) — traditional Han Chinese clothing — has moved from niche cosplay to mainstream fashion among young Chinese. Dedicated hanfu stores exist in every major city. Taobao sells millions of hanfu pieces annually. Universities have hanfu clubs. Some enthusiasts wear it daily.

**What it looks like:** Hanfu is not one style. It spans dynasties and formality levels — from flowing Tang-era robes to structured Ming-era jackets. Modern hanfu often blends historical silhouettes with contemporary fabrics and colors. The effect is neither costume nor fashion — it occupies a category that does not exist in Western clothing culture.

**Why it matters:** Hanfu is part of 国潮 (guochao) — the broader trend of young Chinese embracing traditional Chinese aesthetics. It is not nostalgia. It is identity-building. A generation that grew up with global brands is choosing to wear something that says "this is where I come from."

**What outsiders miss:** Hanfu wearers are not rejecting modernity. They are remixing it. The same person in a Ming-dynasty collar is ordering coffee on her phone and posting the outfit on Xiaohongshu.
