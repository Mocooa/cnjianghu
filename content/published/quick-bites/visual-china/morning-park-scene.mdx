The scene: A public park in any Chinese city, just after dawn.

**What you see:** In one corner, a group of retirees practices tai chi in synchronized silence. Nearby, a woman walks backward along a path — a common exercise believed to benefit the spine. Two men play erhu under a tree, not performing for anyone, just practicing. A cluster of grandmothers does fan dancing. Someone has hung a calligraphy brush from a branch and is writing characters on the pavement with water.

**What you hear:** Birdsong, recorded music from a portable speaker, the rhythmic thwack of a shuttlecock being kicked between four people using only their feet.

**What you feel:** The park is not quiet. It is not chaotic either. It has the organized informality of a place where everyone knows the unwritten schedule — tai chi people get the flat ground, dancers get the pavilion, walkers get the path.

**Why this matters:** In most Western cities, exercise, socializing, and creative practice happen in separate, paid spaces — a gym, a café, a studio. In Chinese parks, all three happen simultaneously, outdoors, for free. The park is not recreation. It is infrastructure for aging well.

**What outsiders miss:** This is not a tourist attraction. It happens every single morning, in every city, with no organization and no admission. The park is China's most underrated public institution.
