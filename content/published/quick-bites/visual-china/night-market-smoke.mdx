The scene: A night market in a second- or third-tier Chinese city, around 9 PM.

**What you see:** Charcoal smoke rises from a row of 烧烤 (shaokao) grills. Metal skewers hold lamb, squid, enoki mushrooms, chicken hearts, and things you cannot identify. Plastic stools line folding tables. Red lanterns or bare bulbs light the scene. A man in a stained apron fans flames with cardboard. Customers point at raw skewers to order — no menu needed.

**What you smell:** Cumin, chili flake, charcoal, hot oil, and the warm sweetness of grilled corn brushed with sauce. Each stall has its own smoke signature. Regulars can identify their favorite vendor by smell alone.

**What you taste:** Everything is seasoned heavily — cumin and chili for the northern-style grill, mala for the Sichuan stalls, oyster sauce and garlic for the Cantonese-leaning vendors. The flavors are loud, unapologetic, and calibrated for beer.

**Why this matters:** The night market is the opposite of restaurant dining. There is no reservation, no interior design, no pretense. Quality is enforced by repeat customers, not reviews. A stall that is not good simply disappears. A stall that is good might run for twenty years.

**What outsiders miss:** The best food at a Chinese night market is never the stall with the longest line of tourists. It is the one where every customer seems to already know the owner.
