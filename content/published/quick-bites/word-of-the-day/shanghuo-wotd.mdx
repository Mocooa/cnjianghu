**上火 (shàng huǒ)** literally means "rising fire." It describes a state where the body feels overheated, irritated, or inflamed — not in a biomedical sense, but in the body-logic framework that shapes everyday Chinese health talk.

**What it feels like:** Someone who says they are 上火 might mean a sore throat, mouth ulcers, breakouts, constipation, or just a general sense of being off. The cause is usually attributed to too much fried food, spicy food, stress, or poor sleep.

**What happens next:** The response is almost always practical. Eat lighter (清淡). Drink herbal tea or chrysanthemum tea. Avoid "heating" foods like lamb, lychee, or fried snacks. Sleep earlier. The point is adjustment, not medication.

**Why outsiders misread it:** 上火 is not a diagnosis you will find in a Western medical textbook. But treating it as superstition misses how it works in practice — as a shared grammar for noticing bodily imbalance and responding with small daily corrections.

**How to use it:** "I've had too much hotpot this week — definitely 上火."
