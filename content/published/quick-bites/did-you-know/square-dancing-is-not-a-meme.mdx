广场舞 (guangchangwu) — public square dancing — is one of the most memed aspects of Chinese daily life. Videos of middle-aged women dancing in synchronized groups get millions of views, usually framed as charming or eccentric. That framing misses almost everything interesting about the practice.

**What is actually happening:** Every evening in Chinese cities, public squares, parks, and residential compound courtyards fill with group dancers. The participants are mostly women over 50, though the age range is widening. They bring portable speakers, follow choreographed routines, and dance for 30-90 minutes. No admission. No instructor fee. No reservation.

**Why it matters:** 广场舞 is exercise, companionship, and a claim on public space all at once. In a country where many older women spent decades focused on family and work, the square becomes the first space that is genuinely theirs. The dancing is social infrastructure.

**The noise controversy:** 广场舞 also generates real conflict. Speakers are loud. Residential neighbors complain. Some cities installed "sound barriers" or designated dancing zones. The controversy itself reveals something important: in Chinese urban life, collective use of public space is a right that has to be negotiated, not something that can be assumed.

**What outsiders miss:** 广场舞 is not a quirk. It shows how health, aging, gender, and public space are woven together in ordinary Chinese city life. If you want to understand Chinese urban rhythms, start here.
