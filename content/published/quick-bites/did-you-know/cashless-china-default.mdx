Visitors to Chinese cities are often stunned: street food vendors, taxi drivers, temple donation boxes, and even beggars use QR codes. Mobile payment is not an option in China. It is the infrastructure.

**How it works:** Nearly all transactions run through two apps — WeChat Pay and Alipay. You scan a QR code (or the vendor scans yours), enter an amount, and the money moves instantly. No card swipe, no PIN, no signature. The entire exchange takes about three seconds.

**Why it happened so fast:** China leapfrogged credit cards. While Western economies built layers of payment infrastructure — checks, then credit cards, then contactless — China went from cash directly to mobile payment. The banking system was less entrenched, smartphones arrived at the right moment, and the government encouraged digital payment for transparency and efficiency.

**What daily life looks like:** Splitting a dinner bill? Everyone scans one person's QR code. Paying rent? WeChat transfer. Buying fruit from a cart with no electricity? The vendor has a laminated QR code taped to a pole. Some younger Chinese have genuinely never used cash in their adult lives.

**What outsiders miss:** Cashless China is not about being high-tech. It is about friction removal. The system works because it is simpler than every alternative — not because Chinese consumers love technology, but because they hate waiting.
