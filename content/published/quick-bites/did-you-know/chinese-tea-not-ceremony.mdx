When foreigners picture Chinese tea culture, they usually imagine a gongfu tea setup: tiny cups, a clay teapot, careful pouring, meditative silence. That world exists. But it is one mode, not the default.

**What daily tea actually looks like:** A glass or a thermos with loose leaves sitting in hot water, refilled throughout the day. No ceremony. No timer. No special table. Office workers, taxi drivers, and grandparents in parks all drink tea this way. The leaves steep continuously — a habit that would horrify a formal tea master but works perfectly for everyday rhythm.

**Why the gap matters:** If you only know Chinese tea through ceremony, you will miss how tea actually lives in China. It is not a special occasion. It is hydration, habit, and mild self-care woven into ordinary hours. Someone sipping chrysanthemum tea at their desk is not performing culture. They are just getting through the afternoon.

**The different registers:** China has at least three tea modes running simultaneously. Casual daily drinking (most common). Social tea sessions with friends or business contacts (middle ground). And formal gongfu tea or competition-grade tea appreciation (specialist). They coexist without conflict — like the difference between making instant coffee, meeting at a café, and attending a cupping session.

**What this tells you:** Chinese tea culture is wide, not tall. Its depth comes from how many people drink tea how often, not from how elaborate the setup can become.
