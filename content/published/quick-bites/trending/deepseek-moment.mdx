**What happened:** In January 2025, DeepSeek — a Chinese AI lab backed by quantitative hedge fund High-Flyer — released open-source models that matched or exceeded the performance of leading Western AI systems. DeepSeek-R1, their reasoning model, competed with OpenAI's best while reportedly being trained at a fraction of the cost.

**Why it shook the industry:** The prevailing narrative was that AI leadership required massive compute budgets, cutting-edge chips, and Silicon Valley-scale investment. DeepSeek challenged all three assumptions. Working under US chip export restrictions, they found architectural innovations that reduced compute requirements dramatically.

**What it revealed:** DeepSeek proved that constraints can drive innovation. When you cannot buy the most powerful chips, you optimize harder. When your budget is smaller, you find more efficient training methods. The result was not a cheap imitation — it was a genuine technical achievement that forced Western labs to reconsider their own approaches.

**The market reaction:** US tech stocks dropped sharply on the news. Nvidia lost hundreds of billions in market value in a single day. The reaction itself was telling — Wall Street had priced AI on the assumption that more hardware always wins. DeepSeek broke that assumption.

**Why it matters:** The DeepSeek moment reframed the AI race. It is not just about who has the most GPUs. It is about who can do the most with what they have. That is a competition China is well-positioned to win.
