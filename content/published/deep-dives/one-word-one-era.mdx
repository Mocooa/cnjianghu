Every December, China's Ministry of Education publishes its list of the year's top ten internet buzzwords. Every December, the grassroots internet produces its own competing list. The two lists rarely agree.

The gap between them is one of the most readable texts in contemporary China.

## 2016: 小确幸 — the year of small joys

The word that defined 2016 was borrowed from Japanese: 小確幸 (xiǎo què xìng), meaning "small but certain happiness." A perfectly brewed cup of coffee. A sunny afternoon with nothing scheduled. The first strawberry of spring.

It was an optimism word. China's GDP was growing at 6.7%. Smartphone adoption was exploding. New apps were launching weekly. The general vibe was: life is getting better, and the evidence is in the small pleasures that are now affordable.

In retrospect, 小确幸 was the last purely optimistic buzzword of the decade.

## 2018: 锦鲤 — the year of magical thinking

<ChineseTerm chinese="锦鲤" pinyin="jǐn lǐ" definition="Koi fish — became slang for extraordinary luck after a viral Alipay giveaway, used to mean 'lucky person'" /> went viral after Alipay ran a National Day promotion where one randomly selected person won a prize package worth millions. The winner was declared China's 锦鲤 — lucky fish. Within days, every platform was running 锦鲤 campaigns. People forwarded koi images on WeChat hoping for luck.

The word captured something real: as the economy's structural opportunities started narrowing, luck started feeling more important than effort. If hard work increasingly led to 内卷, maybe the universe would provide what the job market couldn't.

It was superstition wrapped in internet humor. But the humor had an edge.

## 2020: 内卷 — the year the treadmill sped up

<ChineseTerm chinese="内卷" pinyin="nèi juǎn" definition="Involution — a state of hyper-competition where everyone works harder but nobody advances" glossarySlug="neijuan" /> didn't just enter the chat in 2020 — it consumed it. Originally an academic term from anthropologist Clifford Geertz, it was adopted by Chinese university students to describe the relentless, futile competition they were trapped in.

The Tsinghua bicycle-studying photo made it concrete. But the word's power was its universality. Parents hiring tutors for kindergarteners: 内卷. Companies requiring weekend attendance: 内卷. Students making 80-slide presentations for 5-minute assignments: 内卷.

The word gave a generation its diagnosis. Not "I'm failing" — but "we're all running faster and getting nowhere."

<CulturalNote>
The government's official buzzword list for 2020 included 内卷 — a rare moment of alignment between grassroots internet culture and state-recognized language. By 2021, the word 躺平 (lying flat) would not receive the same official acknowledgment. The gap between government and grassroots word lists widened sharply after 2020.
</CulturalNote>

## 2021: 躺平 — the year of the refusal

<ChineseTerm chinese="躺平" pinyin="tǎng píng" definition="Lying flat — a philosophy of doing the bare minimum, rejecting the hyper-competitive grind" glossarySlug="tangping" /> was 内卷's mirror image. If 内卷 was the problem, 躺平 was the answer: stop competing. Do the minimum. Opt out.

State media rushed to condemn it. Censors removed 躺平 discussion groups. But the word had already embedded itself in the vocabulary. You couldn't un-say it. Every attempt to suppress it confirmed its accuracy — even quitting was not permitted.

## 2022: 摆烂 — the year beyond caring

By 2022, the emotional register shifted again. 摆烂 (bǎi làn) — "let it rot" — lacked 躺平's philosophical dignity. It wasn't a stance or a protest. It was the sound of someone running out of energy to care.

Where 躺平 had a manifesto, 摆烂 had a shrug.

## 2023: 搭子 — the year of the activity buddy

The mood turned a corner. <ChineseTerm chinese="搭子" pinyin="dā zi" definition="Activity buddy — a person you pair with for a specific activity (meals, study, gym) without the obligations of deep friendship" /> described a new social form: low-commitment, purpose-specific companionship. A lunch 搭子 eats with you but doesn't expect weekend hangouts. A gym 搭子 spots you but doesn't attend your birthday.

After years of withdrawal (躺平, 摆烂), 搭子 suggested a careful re-engagement. Not the old social contract — no 人情 debt, no reciprocal obligations — but something lighter. Presence without pressure.

<CulturalNote label="Official vs. grassroots">
Each year, the Ministry of Education's buzzword list leans toward "positive energy" (正能量) words: technological achievements, policy slogans, patriotic terms. The grassroots internet list trends toward emotional honesty: pressure, fatigue, humor, survival. The divergence is itself meaningful — it maps the gap between the China the government narrates and the China that people experience (CNNIC, 2024).
</CulturalNote>

## 2024-25: The words still forming

The current vocabulary is still settling. 发疯文学 (fā fēng wénxué) — "going crazy literature" — describes the trend of young people performatively losing it in work chats, sending absurdist rants to their bosses, or posting deliberately unhinged content. It's not mental illness — it's pressure relief through controlled chaos.

班味 (bān wèi) — "work smell" — describes the lingering aura of corporate life that clings to you even after you leave the office. "I can't get rid of my 班味" means: I have been so consumed by work that it has become my identity, and I hate it.

Both words carry the same DNA as 内卷 and 躺平 — they name experiences that feel too specific for existing vocabulary.

## Reading the decade

Lay the words out chronologically and a story emerges:

| Year | Word | Mood |
|------|------|------|
| 2016 | 小确幸 | Gentle optimism |
| 2018 | 锦鲤 | Hopeful superstition |
| 2020 | 内卷 | Trapped awareness |
| 2021 | 躺平 | Philosophical resistance |
| 2022 | 摆烂 | Exhausted surrender |
| 2023 | 搭子 | Cautious reconnection |
| 2024 | 发疯文学 | Controlled chaos |

It is not a linear decline. It is a wave — pressure building, resistance forming, exhaustion hitting, and then the first tentative steps toward a different kind of engagement.

## The seismograph keeps running

Chinese internet slang is not a curiosity. It is the closest thing to real-time emotional data from a population of 1.1 billion internet users (CNNIC, 2024). Each viral word is a collective decision: "this is what we're feeling, and now we have a name for it."

The words don't change reality. But they change the conversation. And in a society where public discourse is carefully managed, a two-character phrase that goes viral despite the censors might be the most honest speech act available.

The seismograph keeps running. The next word is already forming. It just doesn't have a name yet.
