Line 1 of the Chengdu Metro, a Wednesday afternoon. A young woman in a flowing Tang dynasty dress — cross-collar, wide sleeves, empire waist in celadon green — stands holding a grab bar, scrolling her phone with her free hand. Her hair is pinned up with a jade-colored hairpin. Her sneakers peek out from beneath the hemline.

Nobody stares. The woman across the aisle is also wearing hanfu — a more modest Song dynasty style in muted gray. A teenage girl a few seats down has a Ming dynasty cape over a hoodie. This is Chengdu. This is normal.

## Not one dress, but a dynasty

<ChineseTerm chinese="汉服" pinyin="hàn fú" definition="Traditional Chinese clothing spanning thousands of years of dynastic styles, now revived as a modern fashion and identity movement" glossarySlug="hanfu" /> literally means "clothing of the Han" — but the term covers far more than a single style. It encompasses thousands of years of Chinese dress across dozens of dynasties, each with distinctive silhouettes:

- **Han dynasty (206 BC - 220 AD):** Straight-cut robes (直裾, zhí jū) with a rigid, formal line
- **Tang dynasty (618-907):** Empire-waist dresses (齐胸襦裙, qí xiōng rú qún) with wide sleeves and vivid colors — the showiest era
- **Song dynasty (960-1279):** Restrained, narrow-sleeved robes in muted tones — the intellectual's wardrobe
- **Ming dynasty (1368-1644):** The last pre-Qing dynasty, now the most popular era for hanfu revival, featuring the iconic horse-face skirt

The diversity matters because hanfu is not a costume. It is a design system with regional variants, seasonal adaptations, occasion-specific formality, and aesthetic philosophies that changed every few centuries.

## From forum thread to fashion industry

The modern hanfu movement started in 2003, when a man named Wang Letian walked through the streets of Zhengzhou wearing a hand-sewn Han dynasty robe. Photos circulated on early Chinese internet forums. Some people cheered. Others called him crazy.

For the next decade, hanfu remained a niche subculture — small communities on Baidu Tieba and Douban, annual hanfu gatherings that attracted hundreds, Taobao shops run by enthusiasts in their apartments. The movement was passionate but tiny.

The breakthrough came around 2018-2019, when Douyin's short video format proved that hanfu was irresistibly filmable. A video of a girl spinning in a Tang dynasty dress at a scenic spot could easily get millions of views. The algorithm rewarded the visual drama. The visual drama sold the clothes.

By 2023, the hanfu industry was valued at over ¥10 billion (iiMedia Research, 2023). Taobao listed more than 2,000 dedicated hanfu shops. Professional hanfu photography studios opened in every major city. Chengdu alone had over 50 hanfu rental shops within walking distance of Jinli Ancient Street.

## The 马面裙 moment

The garment that brought hanfu into the mainstream wasn't a flowing robe or a dramatic headdress. It was a skirt.

The <ChineseTerm chinese="马面裙" pinyin="mǎ miàn qún" definition="Horse-face skirt — a Ming dynasty pleated skirt with overlapping front panels, now the most popular hanfu garment for daily wear" glossarySlug="mamianqun" /> is a pleated skirt with overlapping front panels, originally from the Ming dynasty. Its genius for modern adoption is practicality: it pairs naturally with modern tops, works in offices and classrooms, and can be dressed up or down. A 马面裙 with a blouse looks like professional wear with an edge. With a hoodie, it looks casually cool.

In 2022, a controversy turbocharged its popularity. Dior released a skirt that Chinese internet users identified as nearly identical to the 马面裙 silhouette. Dior called it a "new design." Chinese social media erupted. The incident became a cultural flashpoint — not about intellectual property law, but about recognition. The feeling was: the West takes from our design history without even knowing the name of what they're taking.

<CulturalNote>
The Dior 马面裙 controversy (2022) crystallized something the hanfu community had felt for years: Chinese aesthetic traditions are globally influential but rarely credited. After the controversy, 马面裙 sales spiked dramatically — wearing one became not just fashion but a statement. During the 2024 Spring Festival, 马面裙 was the most-searched clothing item on Taobao, outselling jeans in multiple categories (South China Morning Post, 2024).
</CulturalNote>

## The identity question

Hanfu is fashion, but it is never just fashion. Wearing it carries identity weight that a Western observer might not immediately recognize.

For many young Chinese, hanfu is a way of saying: Chinese aesthetics are beautiful, and I want to live inside them — not just visit them in museums. It's a claim on heritage that feels more personal than patriotic. The woman on the Chengdu subway isn't making a political statement. She's wearing what she thinks is beautiful, and her definition of beauty includes the visual vocabulary of her civilization.

But the movement has its tensions. The name 汉服 — "Han clothing" — raises a question: whose clothing? China has 55 officially recognized ethnic minorities, each with their own textile traditions. Some scholars and commentators argue that "hanfu" privileges Han Chinese aesthetics and risks erasing minority dress cultures. Others argue that the term simply refers to historical Chinese clothing broadly.

<CulturalNote label="An unresolved debate">
The hanfu community itself is divided on this question. Purists insist on strict historical accuracy — fabric, cut, construction methods must match documented examples from specific dynasties. Modernizers argue that hanfu should evolve, incorporating contemporary fabrics and silhouettes while maintaining classical proportions. A third camp treats hanfu as inspiration rather than recreation, mixing dynastic elements freely. The debate is heated, detailed, and ongoing — which is itself evidence of how seriously the community takes the tradition.
</CulturalNote>

## The wardrobe as time machine

What makes hanfu different from, say, a Victorian dress revival in England is the scale and the sincerity. This is not reenactment culture. It is not nostalgia cosplay. Millions of young Chinese people are integrating historical dress into their daily wardrobes — wearing 马面裙 to the office, Tang dynasty tops to brunch, Song dynasty robes for wedding photos.

The global spread adds another dimension. Hanfu meetups now happen regularly in Tokyo, London, Paris, New York, and Vancouver — organized by diaspora Chinese and, increasingly, by non-Chinese enthusiasts who encountered the aesthetic on TikTok.

## Opening the wardrobe

Getting dressed in the morning is a daily act so routine it's invisible. But when a 25-year-old in Chengdu opens her closet and reaches past the jeans and the blazer to pull out a Ming dynasty skirt, she's making a choice that carries 3,000 years of aesthetic history into a Tuesday commute.

She doesn't think of it that way. She thinks: this is what I want to wear today.

That's how a revival becomes a reality — not through manifestos or movements, but through the simple, daily, unremarkable act of getting dressed.
