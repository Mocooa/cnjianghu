Here is the spreadsheet.

Column A: Age. Column B: Expected milestone.

- 18: Sit the <ChineseTerm chinese="高考" pinyin="gāo kǎo" definition="China's national university entrance exam — the single test that largely determines university placement and life trajectory" glossarySlug="gaokao" />. Perform well.
- 18–22: University. Preferably a 985 or 211 institution. Graduate on time.
- 22–25: Secure stable employment. Ideally a large company, a civil service post, or something with a clear title.
- 25–28: Begin serious dating. Find someone suitable. Suitable means: similar educational background, comparable family circumstances, employed, with or working toward property.
- 28–30: Marry. Have the apartment sorted, or nearly sorted, before the wedding.
- 30–33: First child.
- Every holiday between 18 and 33: Answer questions about whichever row you are currently failing to fill in.

Nobody gave most Chinese young adults this spreadsheet. They absorbed it, the way you absorb a language — not through formal instruction but through constant ambient exposure. Grandparents who ask about university scores at dinner tables. Parents who send WeChat articles about the fertility advantages of early marriage. Aunts who announce the weddings of your childhood classmates with a particular pointed cheerfulness.

The spreadsheet is not a contract. But the penalties for missing a cell feel real.

## What the script is actually about

Western coverage of Chinese family pressure tends to telescope onto a single point: marriage. The marriage-by-28 expectation is real, but treating it as the whole story misses the architecture around it.

The Chinese life script — or <ChineseTerm chinese="人生规划" pinyin="rén shēng guī huà" definition="Life planning — the culturally expected sequence of milestones (education, career, marriage, home, children) that functions as an unwritten social script" glossarySlug="rensheng-guihua" /> — is a system, not a single pressure point. Each milestone is load-bearing. The 高考 result determines the university. The university determines the job market credibility. The job determines whether you can plausibly save for an apartment. The apartment determines whether a marriage is economically viable. Each cell in the spreadsheet depends on the cells above it.

This is why Chinese family pressure doesn't feel like one conversation. It feels like a permanent audit, with each life stage creating the conditions for the anxiety of the next.

<CulturalNote>
Spring Festival — the Lunar New Year reunion — functions as the script's annual checkpoint. Young adults who haven't met expected milestones describe returning home as running a gauntlet. "Have you found someone?" arrives before the dumplings. "What's the salary like?" before the tea. The questions are not malicious — they are expressions of care in a culture where parents feel responsible for their children's life trajectory well into adulthood. But the cumulative effect, absorbed across a table of aunts and uncles each tracking a different cell in the spreadsheet, is something closer to a performance review than a family dinner.
</CulturalNote>

## The weight at the beginning: 高考

The script's first and perhaps most consequential cell is the 高考. In 2024, 13.42 million students sat this exam. The score they receive — delivered in June, after months of preparation that in many cases spans years — determines which university admits them, which largely determines which employers consider them, which largely determines how the rest of the spreadsheet is even possible to complete.

The phrase that Chinese parents use — 高考改变命运, "gaokao changes fate" — is not hyperbole. It is a rational description of how the system actually works. A student who scores into a top-tier university enters a credential economy that rewards that fact for decades. A student who misses the threshold faces a harder path through a labor market that reads educational pedigree as signal.

This is why the academic pressure that foreign observers sometimes read as parental extremism is better understood as rational risk management inside a system with very high stakes and very low tolerance for course correction.

## The weight in the middle: the housing problem

The spreadsheet's middle rows — stable career, apartment, marriage — create what many young Chinese describe as a structural trap.

In first-tier cities, an apartment costs 40–50 times the average annual income. A ¥5 million apartment in Shanghai requires a ¥1.5 million down payment — a sum that often requires pooling the savings of two parents and sometimes two sets of grandparents. Three generations of accumulation, poured into a down payment, so that the fourth generation can begin the spreadsheet's later cells.

<ChineseTerm chinese="学区房" pinyin="xuéqū fáng" definition="School district housing — an apartment purchased specifically to secure enrollment access to a desirable public school, often at a significant premium" glossarySlug="school-district-housing" /> adds another layer. Good school districts require the right address, which requires property in those districts, purchased before children reach school age. The script, in this sense, runs in parallel tracks: your own 人生规划 and the one you are already planning for children who do not yet exist.

The math has stopped working the way the script assumes it will. University graduates entering the job market in 2023–2024 found a white-collar vacancy rate that didn't match the credential supply. Youth unemployment in mid-2023 hit 21.3% before the government temporarily paused publishing the figure. The "stable career by 25" cell in the spreadsheet increasingly requires luck, connections, or willingness to accept conditions the script didn't mention.

## What <ChineseTerm chinese="内卷" pinyin="nèi juǎn" definition="Involution — hyper-competition where effort escalates without proportional reward, like a treadmill that keeps accelerating" glossarySlug="neijuan" /> does to the timeline

The script assumes a particular relationship between effort and outcome: work hard, complete each stage, advance to the next. <ChineseTerm chinese="内卷" pinyin="nèi juǎn" definition="Involution — hyper-competition where effort escalates without proportional reward" glossarySlug="neijuan" /> is what happens when that relationship breaks down.

When everyone is trying as hard as possible and the number of stable career positions, affordable apartments, and viable marriage candidates hasn't expanded proportionally, the milestones become harder to hit — not because individuals are failing to try, but because the competition for each cell in the spreadsheet has intensified faster than the available outcomes.

A generation that absorbed the script as a promise — study hard, follow the sequence, reach the promised outcomes — is now navigating a system where the promise has become conditional at best. The script still runs. The checkpoints at family dinners still happen. But the gap between what the script expects and what the economy can deliver is widening.

<CulturalNote label="The numbers that changed">
China's marriage rate has fallen for ten consecutive years. In 2013, 13.5 million couples registered to marry. In 2023, that number was 7.68 million — a 43% decline in a decade (Ministry of Civil Affairs, 2023). The average age at first marriage in cities has risen from around 24 to over 29. These are not the numbers of a generation that has abandoned the script's values. They are the numbers of a generation that wants to complete the spreadsheet but is finding row 5 — marriage — increasingly hard to reach, because rows 3 and 4 are jammed.
</CulturalNote>

## Bending the spreadsheet without breaking the family

What does it actually look like when someone decides to deviate?

Not dramatic. That's the first thing. The group chat silence is more common than the confrontational speech. A daughter in Beijing who is 29 and unmarried does not typically announce a philosophy of non-compliance. She deflects at the family dinner. She changes the subject to a work success. She provides her mother with just enough information to maintain hope without committing to a timeline.

This is not dishonesty. It is filial negotiation — the ongoing process of managing parental expectations without severing the relationship. In a culture where <ChineseTerm chinese="孝" pinyin="xiào" definition="Filial piety — the respect and obligation children owe to parents, central to Chinese family ethics" glossarySlug="xiao" /> remains a genuine value, full rebellion against the script carries a moral cost that most young people don't want to pay. The goal is not to destroy the spreadsheet but to quietly edit it — to push back the dates, to renegotiate the sequence, to make visible the conditions that the original script didn't account for.

Some of these negotiations succeed. Parents who initially pressed hard on the marriage question often soften when a child explains, patiently and repeatedly, that the apartment situation makes the timeline unrealistic. Others don't — the parent who frames the unmarried child as a family embarrassment, the family dinner that becomes a genuinely painful event year after year.

The reframe that seems to be emerging, slowly, is this: the script was never really about the milestones. It was about security — the parental desire to see a child reach a life position stable enough to weather whatever comes next. When young people can demonstrate that they are building security on different terms — career stability without a traditional employer, a meaningful relationship without a marriage certificate, a home in a second-tier city rather than a tier-1 apartment — some parents update their model.

Others don't. The generation navigating this knows both types.

## The spreadsheet is still running

The Chinese life script is not dissolving. It is negotiating.

Marriage rates are falling, but most young Chinese adults still expect to marry. Birth rates are declining, but the desire for family hasn't disappeared — the conditions for achieving it have become harder to meet. The gaokao remains the most important single exam in most students' lives, because the structure that makes it important hasn't changed.

What's changing is the assumed timeline, the assumed geography, and the assumed definition of success. A woman who marries at 31 in her hometown, after building a career that sustains her, and who answers the Spring Festival questions with calm rather than shame — she has not failed the script. She has edited it.

The spreadsheet is still running. She just isn't using someone else's column widths.
