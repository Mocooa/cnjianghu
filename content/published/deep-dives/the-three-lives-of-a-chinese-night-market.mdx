Stand at the entrance of Changsha's Pozi Street at 9 PM on a summer Saturday. The air smells like cumin and chili oil. Neon signs pulse in red and gold. A vendor scoops stinky tofu into a paper tray. Behind you, a line of tourists films everything.

This scene looks ancient. Timeless. The kind of street food culture that has existed for centuries.

It hasn't. This specific night market was renovated in 2019 with government funding, designed by urban planners, and promoted on Douyin by a municipal marketing team. The vendors have business licenses, food safety certificates, and assigned stall positions.

The night market you're photographing is on its third life. The first two looked nothing like this.

## Life 1: Survival (1980s-2000s)

After China's economic reforms began in 1978, millions of workers left state-owned enterprises — some voluntarily, many laid off. In the 1990s, as state factories closed across the industrial northeast and interior provinces, an estimated 30 million workers lost their iron rice bowl.

Many turned to the street. A folding table, a gas burner, a bag of ingredients — that was a business. Night markets in this era were not tourist attractions. They were survival infrastructure.

The vendors were laid-off factory workers, rural migrants who couldn't find construction jobs, retirees supplementing pensions that didn't cover rent. The food was cheap because the customers were poor. Locations were informal — a sidewalk near a bus station, a vacant lot beside a residential compound, the shoulder of a road after traffic thinned.

There were no permits. No food safety inspections. No Instagram. The night market existed because people needed to eat, and other people needed to earn. It was the most organic form of economic activity: pure supply meeting pure demand, in the dark, on the street.

## Life 2: Erasure (2005-2018)

Then the cities decided to modernize.

China's urban beautification campaigns — driven by the goal of becoming "civilized cities" (文明城市) — targeted street vendors as visual disorder. The logic was straightforward: world-class cities don't have unlicensed food stalls on their sidewalks. Shanghai cleaned up for the 2010 Expo. Beijing swept vendors before the Olympics. Second-tier cities followed.

The enforcement arm was the <ChineseTerm chinese="城管" pinyin="chéng guǎn" definition="Urban management officers — municipal enforcement officials responsible for street order, often in conflict with unlicensed vendors" />, the urban management officers whose job was to confiscate unlicensed vendor equipment, issue fines, and clear the streets. The 城管-vendor conflict became one of the defining social tensions of the 2010s — viral videos of officers overturning food carts, vendors fleeing with their woks, physical confrontations that occasionally turned violent.

<CulturalNote>
The 城管 paradox captured a deeper contradiction: the government that had liberalized the economy and encouraged entrepreneurship was now criminalizing its most basic form. The vendors weren't criminals — they were doing exactly what the reform era promised: creating livelihoods through individual enterprise. But the beautification agenda required clean streets, and clean streets required their removal.
</CulturalNote>

By 2015, many cities had effectively eliminated informal night markets from their central districts. The street food that tourists romanticized had been pushed to the urban periphery or underground.

## Life 3: Resurrection (2019-present)

Then the economy slowed. And the night markets came back — but this time, by invitation.

In 2019, the State Council issued a policy document encouraging the development of <ChineseTerm chinese="夜经济" pinyin="yè jīng jì" definition="Night economy — a government policy framework promoting nighttime commercial activity as an economic growth driver" glossarySlug="night-economy" /> — the "night economy" — as a strategy to boost consumer spending. Cities were encouraged to designate night market zones, extend business hours, and create "nighttime consumption landmarks."

The COVID pandemic accelerated the pivot. In June 2020, Premier Li Keqiang praised street stall vendors as 人间烟火 — "the fireworks of human life" — and endorsed the 地摊经济 (dìtān jīngjì, stall economy) as a legitimate path to employment recovery. The endorsement went viral. Overnight, the same activity that had been punished for a decade was now promoted as policy (The Paper, 2020).

<CulturalNote label="The numbers behind the night">
The night economy is not marginal. By 2023, nighttime consumption accounted for over 60% of total retail spending in major Chinese cities (Bloomberg, 2023). Cities like Changsha, Chengdu, and Xi'an built their tourism brands explicitly around night markets and nightlife districts. The Changsha municipal government reported that Pozi Street alone generated over ¥200 million in annual revenue.
</CulturalNote>

## Same stalls, different story

The irony is architectural. Stand at a modern night market and you'll see many of the same items that were sold in the 1990s survival markets: grilled skewers, stinky tofu, cold noodles, fried rice. The food hasn't changed much. But the context has transformed completely.

The 1990s vendor was unlicensed, unprotected, and invisible to policy. The 2020s vendor has a QR code for mobile payment, a food hygiene certificate on display, and a stall assignment from the municipal commerce bureau. The customer base has shifted from locals eating cheap dinners to tourists seeking authentic experiences — "authentic" meaning the curated aesthetic of a night market that no longer needs to exist for survival.

The night market went from necessity → nuisance → attraction in thirty years. The same government that demolished it is now marketing it.

## What tourists see, what locals know

The night markets featured on Douyin and 小红书 (Xiaohongshu) are performance spaces — brightly lit, neatly organized, with vendors selected for photogenic food and lively personalities. They are real markets selling real food, but they are also stages.

The local equivalent — the market where residents actually shop for cheap dinners — is usually elsewhere. It's the less photogenic cluster of stalls behind a residential compound, with fluorescent lighting, plastic stools, and prices 40% lower than the tourist zone. It exists in a gray zone of semi-legality, tolerated but not promoted. The city needs it but doesn't want to show it.

Both are night markets. Both are authentic. They just serve different audiences and different functions — and the gap between them is the gap between China's internal reality and its external image.

## The market after midnight

After the tourists leave and the Douyin cameras stop, some night markets keep going. The 2 AM crowd is different — taxi drivers between fares, delivery riders finishing shifts, construction workers eating before dawn starts. The food at this hour is heavier: lamb spine hot pot, fried noodles with extra chili, beer by the case.

This is the oldest version of the night market — the one that predates tourism, policy, and social media. People eating together in the dark because they're hungry, they're tired, and the food is hot.

Three lives, one market, and the smell of cumin hasn't changed.
