Walk into the underground parking garage of any shopping mall in Shenzhen. Start counting license plates by color.

Blue plates: gasoline cars. Green plates: <ChineseTerm chinese="新能源车" pinyin="xīn néng yuán chē" definition="New energy vehicle (NEV) — the Chinese umbrella term for battery electric, plug-in hybrid, and fuel cell vehicles" glossarySlug="new-energy-vehicle" />. In 2020, you'd have counted maybe one green plate for every ten blue ones. In 2025, the ratio is closer to one-to-one. In some newer residential compounds, green plates are the majority.

This is not a government showroom or a tech company campus. This is an ordinary parking garage. The transition happened here — in the most mundane space imaginable — and if you weren't watching, you missed the moment it tipped.

## The numbers that broke the curve

In 2024, China sold over 10 million new energy vehicles — more than half of all new cars sold in the country (CAAM, 2024). That's not a niche. That's the new normal.

For context: the entire European Union sold about 3 million EVs in the same year. The United States sold about 1.4 million. China sold more EVs than the rest of the world combined.

The growth curve was not gradual. NEV market share went from 5% in 2020 to 15% in 2022 to 35% in 2023 to over 50% in 2024. The S-curve that technology analysts predicted would take a decade compressed into four years.

## Why this happened (it's not the planet)

The standard narrative — consumers chose EVs to fight climate change — is mostly wrong for China. The actual drivers were pragmatic:

**License plates.** In Shanghai, a gasoline car license plate costs around ¥100,000 and requires years on a waiting list. An NEV plate is free and issued immediately. In Beijing, the gasoline plate lottery has odds of roughly 0.5%. The NEV queue is much shorter. For millions of urban Chinese, the choice between waiting five years for a gas car plate and getting an EV plate next month is not about the environment. It's about transportation right now.

**Total cost of ownership.** Electricity in China costs roughly ¥0.5-0.8 per kWh. Charging an EV costs about ¥30-50 for a full charge that covers 400+ km. The equivalent gasoline cost is ¥200-300. Over three years, the fuel savings alone can exceed ¥30,000. When the purchase price is similar (and for many models, EVs are now cheaper than comparable gas cars), the math is overwhelming.

**The cars got good.** Early Chinese EVs were cheap and looked it. Then BYD, NIO, Xpeng, and Li Auto started competing on design, technology, and driving experience. BYD's Han sedan has a range of 600+ km, a 0-100 km/h time of 3.9 seconds, and an interior that reviewers compare favorably to Mercedes. It costs ¥200,000 — about $27,000.

<CulturalNote>
The green license plate has become an unexpected social signifier. In first-tier cities, a green plate no longer signals "budget car" — it signals "smart buyer" or "tech-forward." Among younger buyers, green plates carry more status than blue ones. The cultural inversion is complete: the old-money signal (imported German sedan with a blue plate) is being displaced by the new-money signal (domestic EV with a green plate and a massive center screen).
</CulturalNote>

## The BYD story

No company embodies the transition better than BYD (比亚迪). Founded in 1995 as a rechargeable battery manufacturer — making phone batteries for Motorola and Nokia — BYD entered the automotive market in 2003 by acquiring a failing state car company.

For fifteen years, BYD made unremarkable cars. The early models were cheap, utilitarian, and uninspiring. Industry analysts dismissed the company regularly.

Then three things converged: BYD's battery expertise gave it a structural cost advantage (it manufactures its own Blade Battery cells), the Chinese EV market reached critical mass, and BYD's design team finally produced cars people wanted to look at.

In 2024, BYD sold over 4 million vehicles globally, surpassing Toyota in quarterly sales for the first time (Reuters, 2025). A phone battery company became the world's largest EV manufacturer. The story is implausible and well-documented.

## The infrastructure underneath

An EV transition at this scale requires more than good cars. It requires infrastructure — and China has built it at a pace that mirrors its high-speed rail expansion.

By the end of 2024, China had over 3 million public charging points — more than the rest of the world combined (IEA, 2024). The distribution is dense: in tier-1 cities, finding a public charger is roughly as easy as finding a gas station. Highway rest stops have fast-charging banks with 20+ stalls. Shopping malls have chargers in their parking garages. Many residential compounds have installed community charging stations.

<ChineseTerm chinese="换电站" pinyin="huàn diàn zhàn" definition="Battery swap station — a facility where EVs can exchange depleted batteries for fully charged ones in minutes" glossarySlug="battery-swap" /> is the other model. NIO, the premium Chinese EV brand, operates over 2,500 battery swap stations where drivers pull in, an automated system removes the depleted battery from the car's chassis, and installs a fully charged one. Total time: about 5 minutes. Faster than pumping gas.

<CulturalNote label="Range anxiety is over">
The phrase "range anxiety" — the fear that an EV will run out of charge before reaching a charger — has largely disappeared from Chinese consumer conversations. A 2024 survey by JD Power found that range anxiety ranked seventh among EV purchase concerns in China, behind factors like interior quality and infotainment systems. The infrastructure caught up, and the worry moved on to other things.
</CulturalNote>

## The supply chain question

China's EV dominance is not just about selling cars domestically. It controls approximately 75% of the global lithium-ion battery supply chain — from mining (or securing mining contracts in Africa and South America) to refining to cell manufacturing to pack assembly (Financial Times, 2024).

This means that even EVs sold by European and American brands often contain Chinese-made battery cells. The supply chain concentration has become a geopolitical issue, with the EU and US imposing tariffs and localisation requirements. But the structural advantage — built over two decades of industrial policy and manufacturing investment — is difficult to replicate quickly.

## What the parking lot says

Walk back into that Shenzhen parking garage. The green plates aren't a government program's success metric. They're not an environmentalist's victory. They're the visible evidence of millions of individual decisions made for personal, practical reasons: this car is cheaper to run, easier to license, and nicer to drive.

The transition didn't happen because people were convinced. It happened because the alternative became obviously better. The parking lot doesn't lie — it's full of green plates because green plates won on price, on convenience, and increasingly, on desirability.

That's not a prediction about the future. It's a photograph of the present. The future is already parked.
