In 2020, a photo circulated on Chinese social media: a Tsinghua University student pedaling an exercise bike while balancing a laptop on the handlebars, typing a paper. Other students were studying at desks around him. Nobody looked up. The image became a meme overnight, captioned with two characters that would define the year: 内卷.

By 2021, a different image circulated: a man lying on a bare mattress in an empty apartment, posting a manifesto about doing the minimum. Two characters: 躺平.

By 2022, there was no image — just a phrase that spread like exhaled smoke: 摆烂. Let it rot.

Three words. Three years. One story.

## Act 1: 内卷 — the race that nobody wins (2020)

<ChineseTerm chinese="内卷" pinyin="nèi juǎn" definition="Involution — a state of hyper-competition where everyone works harder but nobody advances, like running on a treadmill that speeds up" glossarySlug="neijuan" /> entered mainstream vocabulary in 2020, but the feeling it named was older. The word originally came from anthropologist Clifford Geertz's study of Javanese rice farming — a society that kept intensifying its cultivation methods without ever increasing its output. Chinese academics borrowed the term. Then the internet made it viral.

The scenarios were instantly recognizable. A job posting for a mid-level marketing position receives 3,000 applications. Elementary school parents hiring three tutors for a child who already scores 95 out of 100. Tech workers staying at the office until midnight — not because they have work, but because the team lead hasn't left yet.

内卷 captured something that "competition" alone could not: the futility. Everyone understood that the extra effort wasn't producing extra results. The treadmill was speeding up, but nobody was getting anywhere. The word gave a generation permission to name the trap.

<CulturalNote>
The structural context behind 内卷 is measurable. In 2024, a record 13.42 million students sat for the <ChineseTerm chinese="高考" pinyin="gāo kǎo" definition="China's national college entrance exam — a single test that largely determines university placement and life trajectory" glossarySlug="gaokao" />, competing for university seats that haven't expanded proportionally. Youth unemployment peaked at 21.3% in mid-2023 before the government temporarily stopped publishing the figure (Financial Times, 2023). The effort-to-outcome ratio was objectively collapsing.
</CulturalNote>

## Act 2: 躺平 — the refusal to run (2021)

If 内卷 was the diagnosis, <ChineseTerm chinese="躺平" pinyin="tǎng píng" definition="Lying flat — a philosophy of doing the bare minimum as a deliberate rejection of hyper-competitive culture" glossarySlug="tangping" /> was the first response.

In April 2021, a Sichuan factory worker named Luo Huazhong posted a now-deleted essay on Baidu Tieba titled "躺平即是正义" — "Lying flat is justice." He described quitting his job, cutting expenses to nearly zero, and doing occasional odd work to survive. He ate two meals a day, exercised in the park, and read philosophy.

The post went nuclear. Within weeks, 躺平 was the most discussed concept on Chinese social media. It was not laziness — it was philosophical. If the game is rigged, the rational move is to stop playing. If running faster doesn't get you ahead, lying down is not defeat. It's clarity.

The government noticed. State media published editorials criticizing 躺平 as irresponsible. Censors scrubbed 躺平 groups from social platforms. A People's Daily commentary declared that "lying flat is shameful." The irony was not lost on anyone: the backlash proved the point. Even quitting quietly was not allowed.

<CulturalNote label="Kong Yiji literature">
In 2023, the concept evolved into <ChineseTerm chinese="孔乙己文学" pinyin="kǒng yǐ jǐ wén xué" definition="Kong Yiji literature — a trend where overqualified young graduates compare themselves to a failed scholar from a Lu Xun story" /> — named after a character in Lu Xun's fiction who is too educated to do manual labor but too poor to join the elite. Young graduates with master's degrees delivering food on scooters called themselves "modern Kong Yijis." The literary reference made the pain harder to censor.
</CulturalNote>

## Act 3: 摆烂 — beyond protest, into weather (2022)

<ChineseTerm chinese="摆烂" pinyin="bǎi làn" definition="Let it rot — an attitude of complete disengagement, beyond even the philosophical resistance of lying flat" glossarySlug="bailan" /> arrived quietly. Where 躺平 was a stance — deliberate, articulated, almost heroic — 摆烂 had no manifesto. It was not protest. It was not philosophy. It was simply... stopping.

The word comes from basketball slang, describing a team that tanks the season deliberately to get a better draft pick. But as it spread through Chinese internet culture, it lost its strategic edge. 摆烂 became the mood of a generation that had moved past anger into something flatter: indifference.

A student submits a half-finished thesis. A worker responds to every email with a single emoji. A job seeker stops sending applications. Not because they're making a point — because they've run out of points to make.

The progression is precise:
- **内卷**: "The system demands too much" (recognition)
- **躺平**: "I refuse to comply" (resistance)
- **摆烂**: "I don't care anymore" (exhaustion)

## The silence between the words

What makes this sequence significant is not the individual words — every generation complains about work. It's the speed and universality of the progression. Three words, three consecutive years, each one representing a deeper withdrawal from the social contract that had defined Chinese aspiration for decades: study hard → get into a good university → get a stable job → buy an apartment → marry → have children → prosper.

Each link in that chain has weakened. University graduates outnumber white-collar jobs. Apartments in first-tier cities cost 40-50 times annual income. Marriage rates have declined for nine consecutive years. The birth rate hit a historic low in 2023.

The three words are not causes of these trends. They are names for the emotional experience of living through them.

## What the government hears

Beijing's response has been telling. 内卷 was tolerated as social commentary. 躺平 was actively censored. 摆烂 is mostly ignored — which may be the most unsettling response of all, because it suggests the authorities don't know what to do with apathy.

You can argue with resistance. You can punish dissent. But disengagement — a generation that simply shrugs — that's harder to govern.

## After the trilogy

The question Chinese commentators are asking now is: what comes after 摆烂? Is the next word in the sequence darker, or does the cycle reset?

Some signs point toward reset. The rise of 搭子 (dāzi) culture — finding "activity buddies" for specific interests rather than deep friendships — suggests young people are rebuilding social connections on smaller, lower-stakes terms. Not ambition, but presence. Not career goals, but afternoon coffee.

Three words walked a generation from exhaustion to refusal to silence. Whatever word comes next won't just be slang. It will be a weather report from inside a billion lives.
