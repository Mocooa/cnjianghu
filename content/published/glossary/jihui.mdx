## What kind of word this is

忌讳 describes things that are avoided, not because they are illegal or morally wrong, but because they carry bad association or invite misfortune. The avoidances operate through sound, color, shape, and timing — often at the level of instinct rather than conscious calculation.

## How it works in practice

Some of the most common 忌讳 in everyday life:

- **The number 4** — 四 (sì) sounds like 死 (death). Buildings commonly skip the 4th floor; phone numbers and license plates ending in 4 are less desirable.
- **Green hats** — wearing a green hat signals that one's wife has been unfaithful, due to a historical association. The phrase "戴绿帽子" is an insult.
- **Clocks as gifts** — 送钟 (sòng zhōng) sounds like 送终, attending someone's death. Giving a clock as a gift is broadly avoided.
- **White and black at celebrations** — both colors carry mourning associations and are inappropriate for weddings and New Year.
- **Umbrella at housewarming** — 伞 (sǎn) sounds like 散, to scatter or disperse.

## Where the avoidances cluster

忌讳 concentrates around the most important transitions: marriages, births, deaths, New Year, and business openings. The more significant the occasion, the more carefully the 忌讳 are observed. Businesses pay substantial premiums for auspicious phone numbers and addresses.

## What outsiders often miss

忌讳 is not irrational fear. It functions as a shared grammar of care — choosing words, colors, and numbers that show you thought about the occasion and avoided bringing ill-fitting associations into it. Ignoring 忌讳 at a wedding or funeral signals inattention or disrespect.

## Why it matters

忌讳 shapes purchases, addresses, gift choices, business naming, and building design across China at scale. Understanding what is avoided — and why — makes Chinese social choices legible in a way that simply knowing what is preferred does not.
