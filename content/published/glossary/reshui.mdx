## What it means in context

热水 is the default drink in China. Restaurants serve it before you order. Hospitals recommend it after treatment. Hotels provide electric kettles in every room. The assumption that a person would want hot or at least warm water — not cold, not room temperature — is built into the infrastructure of daily life.

## The phrase everyone knows

多喝热水 (drink more hot water) is so universal it became a meme. It is the advice people give for headaches, colds, menstrual cramps, bad moods, and general malaise. The phrase is affectionate and exasperating in equal measure — well-meaning but so generic that it has become a joke about the limits of Chinese-style concern. Boyfriends, in particular, are mocked for offering it as a cure-all.

## What outsiders often miss

The preference for hot water is not random and not merely about taste. It is grounded in Chinese medical thinking that cold liquids can damage the 脾胃 (digestive system) and that warmth supports the body's internal balance. Cold water is thought to slow digestion, constrict circulation, and introduce unwanted 寒气 (cold qi) into the body. Whether or not you subscribe to the theory, the practice shapes daily life from breakfast to bedtime.

## How it shapes routine

The hot water habit means kettles are boiled multiple times a day in most households. Thermoses are carried everywhere. Ice is used sparingly even in summer. Many Chinese travelers abroad are genuinely uncomfortable when restaurants serve ice water by default — it feels, within their body logic, like a mildly harmful act.

## Why it matters

热水 is one of the simplest windows into how Chinese health logic differs from Western defaults. It is not exotic or mysterious — it is just a different set of assumptions about what the body needs, applied consistently across an entire culture's daily habits.
