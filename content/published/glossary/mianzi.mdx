## Beyond the literal

面子 is often translated as "face," but that can sound like private pride or personal ego. 面子 is more public than that. It is about how someone stands in front of others.

## How it works

面子 is something people can **give**, **leave**, **save**, or **lose**.

- **给面子** — to show respect publicly, to honor someone's standing
- **留面子** — to avoid embarrassing someone in front of others
- **没面子 / 丢面子** — to be embarrassed, diminished, or publicly slighted

It shapes how praise, refusal, criticism, joking, and even generosity are performed.

## Where it shows up

- Banquets and toasts
- Workplace hierarchy
- Family negotiations
- Public disagreement
- Business hospitality

## What outsiders often miss

面子 is not just vanity. It is part of how relationships remain workable. A direct move that feels "honest" in one culture can feel unnecessarily damaging in another if it publicly strips someone of standing.

## Why it matters

Understanding 面子 helps explain why Chinese social life can look indirect, ceremonious, or overly careful from the outside. Often the point is not to avoid truth, but to manage truth in a way that keeps the relationship usable afterwards.
