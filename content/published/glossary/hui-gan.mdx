## More than sweetness

回甘 is often translated as returning sweetness or sweet aftertaste. That is roughly correct, but the term matters because it describes a timing of taste, not just a flavor note.

The sweetness is felt after bitterness, dryness, or restraint, which is why it often carries evaluative weight in tea talk.

## What people usually mean

When people talk about 回甘, they are usually noticing:

- sweetness that arrives after swallowing
- a lingering pleasant finish
- the sense that a tea opens up gradually rather than all at once

That is why the term often sounds more process-oriented than a simple adjective like "sweet."

## What outsiders often miss

回甘 is not only a technical tasting note for experts. It helps explain how many Chinese drinkers talk about quality through sequence and after-effect rather than immediate impact alone.

## Why it matters

If foreigners want to understand why Chinese tea language often sounds so attentive to pacing and lingering sensation, 回甘 is one of the key terms.
