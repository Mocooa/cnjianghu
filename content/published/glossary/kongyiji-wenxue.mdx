## What kind of term this is

孔乙己文学 is a genre of online self-expression that emerged in 2023. It takes its name from a character in a short story by Lu Xun, widely considered modern China's most important writer. The term spread rapidly on social media as young graduates recognized their own situation in a fictional figure from a century earlier.

## Who Kong Yiji is

孔乙己 is a character from Lu Xun's 1919 story of the same name. He is a poor, failed scholar who insists on wearing his long scholarly robe even as he drinks at a cheap tavern and is mocked by the other patrons. He speaks in classical Chinese phrases that nobody around him uses. He cannot let go of his identity as an educated man, even though that identity has brought him nothing. The story treats him with both sympathy and merciless clarity.

## What people usually mean

When Chinese graduates post 孔乙己文学, they are saying: I spent years earning a degree, and now I cannot find work that matches it. But I also cannot bring myself to take a manual job or service position, because my education has become part of how I see myself. The "long scholarly robe" is the degree they cannot take off. The posts are usually self-mocking, mixing real frustration with literary irony.

## What outsiders often miss

孔乙己文学 is not simply self-pity or complaint. By invoking Lu Xun, young Chinese writers place their frustration within a tradition of intellectuals critiquing social structures. Lu Xun used Kong Yiji to diagnose a system that produced scholars with no useful role. A century later, graduates are using the same character to diagnose a system that produces diplomas faster than it produces matching careers. The literary reference is the point — it transforms personal grievance into social commentary with historical depth.

## Why it matters

孔乙己文学 joins 内卷, 躺平, and 摆烂 as another keyword for understanding how Chinese youth process economic pressure through language. Each term captures a different posture — relentless competition, refusal to compete, letting things rot, and now the inability to shed an identity that no longer serves you. Together, they form a vocabulary of structural frustration that is sharpened, not dulled, by humor.
