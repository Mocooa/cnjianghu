## What kind of idea this is

人生规划 translates literally as "life planning," but in practice it describes something more like a script than a plan. The milestones are largely predetermined by social expectation: pass 高考, get into a good university, find stable employment, marry by a certain age, buy property, have children. Deviations invite questions.

## How the script runs

The sequence is roughly:

- **18–22**: university, ideally a ranked one
- **22–25**: enter a stable career, preferably in a major city
- **25–28**: begin serious dating toward marriage
- **28–30**: marriage, with the expectation of homeownership soon after
- **30–35**: first child

Family gatherings — particularly Spring Festival — function as informal checkpoints. Older relatives ask about each stage and register concern when the timeline slips.

## Where the pressure concentrates

The script creates the most friction around marriage timing and property. In many families, having a job but no apartment or no partner past thirty signals something has gone wrong. Young adults describe navigating family dinners that feel like performance reviews.

## What outsiders often miss

人生规划 is not always experienced as oppressive. Many young people genuinely want the things on the list. The tension arises when the script's timeline collides with economic reality — when apartments cost thirty years of salary, or when stable careers are harder to secure than the script assumed.

## Why it matters

Understanding 人生规划 helps explain the intensity of Chinese youth culture debates around 躺平, late marriage, and not having children. The refusal or delay of each milestone is legible as a statement precisely because the script is so well understood.
