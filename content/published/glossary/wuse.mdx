## What kind of idea this is

五色 — the five canonical colors — are not simply a palette. They are a map. Each color in the system corresponds to a direction, a season, an organ, an element, and a social meaning. The system is part of the broader framework of 五行 (wuxing, the five phases), which orders Chinese cosmology.

## The five colors and their correspondences

| Color | Chinese | Direction | Season | Element |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Blue-green | 青 (qīng) | East | Spring | Wood |
| Red | 赤 (chì) | South | Summer | Fire |
| Yellow | 黄 (huáng) | Center | — | Earth |
| White | 白 (bái) | West | Autumn | Metal |
| Black | 黑 (hēi) | North | Winter | Water |

Yellow, as the color of the center and earth, was reserved for emperors. Wearing yellow without rank was a serious transgression. Red carries auspiciousness and celebration. White is associated with mourning. These meanings still run live in contemporary Chinese life.

## Where it shows up

- Festival and wedding color choices (red) and avoidances (white)
- Imperial architecture, where yellow roof tiles signal dynastic authority
- TCM diagnosis, where complexion colors index internal organ states
- Contemporary design, where 五色 logic informs what "feels right" or wrong for an occasion

## What outsiders often miss

The 五色 system is not about decoration. It is about correspondence — the idea that the visible world maps onto invisible structural relationships. A color is never neutral; it always carries a position in the larger order.

## Why it matters

Understanding 五色 explains why color choices in China — for weddings, gifts, architecture, and branding — carry weight that seems disproportionate to Western eyes. Color here is never just aesthetic preference. It is relational and sometimes structural.
