## More than "polite"

客气 is often translated as polite or courteous, but that is only the surface. In real Chinese use, the word helps describe how much distance, restraint, or ceremonial courtesy is being maintained between people.

That is why it can sometimes sound positive and sometimes slightly critical.

## What people usually mean

When people say someone is 客气, they may mean:

- they are being properly courteous
- they are refusing too insistently
- they are keeping too much distance
- they are not acting with enough ease or familiarity

You can even hear 不要客气, which does not only mean "please." It often means "drop the extra formality and come closer."

## What outsiders often miss

客气 is not just etiquette. It helps manage social distance. Being too 客气 can suggest that a relationship is still formal, guarded, or not yet warm enough.

That is why hosts in China often tell guests not to be 客气 while continuing to offer food, tea, or fruit.

## Why it matters

If foreigners want to understand why Chinese hospitality can feel warm and slightly pressuring at the same time, 客气 is one of the most useful words to know.
