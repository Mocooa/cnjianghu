## What this page is

This is better understood as a **concept note** than as a single natural Chinese term. Foreign readers often hear phrases like "first-tier," "new first-tier," or "lower-tier," but those labels do not all come from the same official system.

## What is official

China does have official ways of classifying cities, but they do not map neatly onto internet or media talk about "tiers."

One important official baseline is the State Council's 2014 notice on city-size classification, which uses **urban resident population in built-up areas** to divide cities into categories such as small, medium, large, megacity, and super-megacity.

## What is widely used but not official

The language of **first-tier**, **new first-tier**, **second-tier**, and so on is widely used in media, business, recruiting, and everyday speech. But labels like **new first-tier city** are not national administrative ranks.

In recent years, one of the best-known public frameworks has been Yicai's annual **Ranking of Cities' Attractiveness in China**, which evaluates cities using a methodology based on several indices such as commercial concentration, connectivity, resident activity, new-economy competitiveness, and future potential.

## What people usually mean in conversation

When people in China talk about city tier, they are often compressing several things at once:

- job opportunity
- housing pressure
- prestige
- speed of life
- marriage-market expectations
- what kind of future seems available there

That is why "city tier" in conversation often behaves less like a statistic and more like a social shorthand.

## What outsiders often miss

It is inaccurate to treat city-tier language as a single official hierarchy issued by the state. It is more accurate to separate:

- official population- or administration-based classifications
- influential but non-official ranking systems
- everyday social usage
