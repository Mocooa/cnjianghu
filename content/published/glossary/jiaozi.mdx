## What kind of food this is

饺子 are crescent-shaped dumplings made from a thin wheat wrapper folded around a filling, usually pork and cabbage, though variations are endless. They are boiled, steamed, or pan-fried depending on region and preference. As an everyday food, they are common across northern China. But their deepest cultural significance comes at Spring Festival.

## Why they matter at New Year

饺子 are folded to resemble ancient gold ingots (元宝), so eating them carries a wish for prosperity. Their name is a near-homophone of 交子 — the moment when the old year becomes the new. This is why families in northern China eat them at midnight on New Year's Eve: the dumplings mark the year's most important threshold. Timing is not incidental; it is the point.

## What outsiders often miss

Making 饺子 is a family activity, not just cooking. The process of mixing filling, rolling wrappers, and folding dumplings together is the ritual itself. Three generations sit around a table, each with a task. Some families hide a coin inside one dumpling — whoever finds it in their bowl gets extra luck for the year. The communal labor is as meaningful as the eating.

## Regional and everyday context

饺子 are primarily a northern Chinese tradition. Southern families are more likely to eat 汤圆 or 年糕 at New Year. In daily life, 饺子 are street food, home cooking, and frozen convenience food all at once. But even people who eat them weekly treat the New Year batch differently — the ingredients are chosen more carefully, the folding is done together, and the timing is deliberate.

## Why it matters

饺子 are where food, family, and time converge. They are among China's most culturally loaded foods, and understanding why requires looking beyond the recipe to the occasion, the process, and the symbolism built into every fold.
