## What kind of word this is

上火 is everyday body language, not a modern medical diagnosis. People use it to describe a body that feels irritated, overheated, or thrown off.

## What people usually mean

Depending on context, 上火 can point to:

- sore throat
- mouth ulcers
- breakouts
- constipation
- irritability
- a general sense that the body is running too "hot"

People may say they are 上火 after too much fried food, too much spicy food, not enough sleep, long stress, or travel and overwork.

## What usually follows

Once someone says 上火, the next move is rarely abstract. It usually leads to practical adjustments:

- eating more 清淡 food
- drinking more water or tea believed to cool the body
- sleeping earlier
- avoiding foods thought to be too heating

## What outsiders often miss

上火 is not best read as a precise disease category. It is closer to a shared everyday grammar for noticing bodily imbalance before anyone reaches for a more formal explanation.

## Why it matters

If you want to understand daily Chinese health talk, 上火 matters because it shows that many ordinary health decisions are still organized through lived body logic rather than through clinical language alone.
