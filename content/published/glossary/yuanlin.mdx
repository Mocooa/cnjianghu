## What kind of concept this is

园林 refers to the art of Chinese garden design, but the English word "garden" is misleading if you picture European lawns and symmetry. A classical Chinese garden is not a display to be admired from a terrace. It is an environment to be moved through, where the experience changes with every step.

## How it works in practice

A 园林 is designed so that every few steps, the view shifts. A window frames a bamboo grove. A path curves to reveal a pond. A rockery blocks the sightline and then releases it. Corridors, moon gates, and lattice screens divide the space into a sequence of encounters rather than a single open field. The designer controls not just what you see, but when and how you see it.

## What outsiders often miss

园林 is spatial storytelling. The garden designer is choreographing your experience of time and surprise, much as a film director controls pacing. This is why the famous gardens of Suzhou earned their UNESCO World Heritage status — not for their botanical collections, but for the sophistication of their spatial design. The logic is cinematic before cinema existed.

## Why it matters

Understanding 园林 helps explain a broader pattern in Chinese aesthetics: the preference for sequence over symmetry, concealment over display, and suggestion over full disclosure. These principles appear not only in gardens but in painting, poetry, architecture, and even contemporary Chinese interior design.

## Where you encounter it

The best-known 园林 are in Suzhou and Yangzhou, though examples exist across southern China. The Humble Administrator's Garden, the Lingering Garden, and the Master of the Nets Garden are among the most studied. Visiting one without understanding the underlying design logic is like watching a film with the sound off — you see the images, but miss the rhythm.
