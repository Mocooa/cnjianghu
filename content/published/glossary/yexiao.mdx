## More than eating after dark

邏런쉇 is often translated as late-night snack, but in Chinese life it can point to a whole after-hours rhythm: overtime ending late, friends lingering outside, students still awake, or a city that does not feel done yet.

The term is small, but the social world around it is large.

## Where it shows up

You are likely to hear 邏런쉇 in scenes like:

- after-work meals that happen too late to count as dinner
- delivery orders placed after study or overtime
- barbecue, noodles, or skewers after a night out
- a casual suggestion to keep the evening going rather than go home

## What outsiders often miss

邏런쉇 is not just a snack category. It often implies timing, companionship, release, and urban looseness.

That is why 邏런쉇 can feel more recreational and social than the English phrase "late-night snack" suggests.

## Why it matters

If daytime food in China often reveals routine, 邏런쉇 reveals what people do once routine loosens: rest together, wander, recover, and keep the city alive a little longer.
