## What this page is

学区房 is a real and common term, so it stays a **term** here. But it needs a data-aware explanation because people use it to talk about housing, education access, policy design, and family strategy all at once.

## What it refers to

At the most basic level, 学区房 means housing whose value is linked, formally or informally, to school-admission expectations. People use the term when they believe a location may improve access to more desirable compulsory-education options.

## What is official and what is not

The official baseline is not "buy this home and you automatically get this school forever." Chinese education authorities emphasize **免试就近入学** and have repeatedly tried to curb school-district-housing speculation through measures such as multi-school zoning, tighter enrollment rules, and more balanced resource distribution.

So the term is real, but the relationship between housing and school access is shaped by changing local policy rather than one fixed national formula.

## What people usually mean in conversation

In everyday use, 学区房 often compresses:

- parental anxiety about educational opportunity
- willingness to spend heavily for perceived stability
- housing as a tool of family strategy
- the belief that location can change a child's competitive path

That is why the phrase carries more pressure than a simple real-estate label.

## What outsiders often miss

It is too simple to read 学区房 as only a property-market oddity. It sits at the intersection of education competition, urban inequality, family planning, and policy attempts to rebalance access.

## What we should not overclaim

学区房 does not mean there is one universal national rule linking one apartment to one school. Local implementation differs, and official policy has often tried to weaken rigid one-home-one-school expectations. But the term still matters because it reveals how deeply education pressure shapes housing decisions in China.
