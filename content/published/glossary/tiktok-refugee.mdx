## What this page is

`TikTok Refugee` works best here as a **concept note**. It was a real label that circulated widely in January 2025, but it names a cross-platform migration moment more than it names one stable Chinese everyday term.

## Event baseline

The phrase took off when large numbers of English-speaking TikTok users moved onto Xiaohongshu, also called RedNote, as a possible U.S. TikTok ban approached in January 2025. News coverage and user self-description both helped popularize the label.

## What people usually mean

In practice, the phrase pointed to more than account migration. It also described:

- sudden cross-language platform contact
- Americans learning Chinese platform etiquette in public
- Chinese users reacting to a new foreign influx on a domestic-first app
- the realization that China-side apps are not just clones of Western ones

That is why the phrase became useful inside cnjianghu's `Cross-Border App Encounters` dossier.

## What outsiders often miss

`TikTok Refugee` was not just a funny migration meme. It exposed a real platform-culture shock: moderation expectations, app etiquette, translation gaps, and different assumptions about what a social platform is for.

## What we should not overclaim

The phrase does not describe every foreign user on Xiaohongshu, and it should not be mistaken for a stable long-term identity category. But as a moment label, it captures one of the clearest recent encounters between English-speaking internet culture and a major Chinese platform world.
