## What people usually mean

铁饭碗 refers to a job that feels secure enough to rely on for the long term. Historically it is closely associated with state-sector employment, but in current Chinese use it can still function more broadly as shorthand for durable, low-risk work.

## Why the image is so strong

The phrase combines two ideas:

- the rice bowl as livelihood
- iron as something hard to break

That is why the term still carries emotional force even when the labor market has changed a great deal.

## What outsiders often miss

铁饭碗 is not only about laziness, bureaucracy, or lack of ambition. In Chinese social life it often points to security, family reassurance, and future legibility. People may want a 铁饭碗 not because they reject change, but because they are trying to reduce life risk.

## Why it matters

If foreigners want to understand why work stability in China can sound like a moral virtue as much as a career preference, 铁饭碗 is one of the clearest enduring terms.
